Detached, Disconnected

I Thought It Was Fun, Then I Asked Why.

There’s a kind of distance I feel from things sometimes. Not physically, but emotionally and mentally. I’m not angry about it, and I don’t hate people. I just seem to experience things differently. A lot of the time, it feels like I’m observing moments instead of fully being inside them.

The more I pay attention, the more patterns I notice. People seem able to disappear into experiences that leave me standing outside of them, observing instead of reacting. Over time, I’ve realized I respond to life differently than most people around me.

Sometimes I sit quietly and watch people scream on roller coasters. They laugh, shout, film it like it’s the highlight of their lives. They wait in line, pay money, call it “fun.”

I don’t experience it the same way. From where I’m standing, it looks like controlled fear briefly transformed into excitement. The body spikes with adrenaline, panic turns into laughter for a minute, then everything settles back to normal again.

And then they go home saying it was the best part of their day.

People chase these highs through lights, noise, movement, crowds, again and again. It starts looking like the same pattern repeated in different forms. One person finds it on a ride, another in a festival crowd, another in shopping.

I notice myself finding something different in quieter spaces, in music, in being alone. Not better or worse than what other people enjoy. Just quieter.

And I don’t fully know whether that means I understand these experiences differently, or just experience them differently.

I haven’t really been able to fully join the rush. Even when I’m physically present, part of me stays half a step outside the moment. While something is happening, I often catch myself questioning it in real time. What makes it feel meaningful? Why do people chase it so relentlessly? Why are so many experiences built around temporary spikes of excitement? Sometimes I wonder whether it’s really joy, or just relief from boredom wearing a brighter face.

Maybe that’s the difference. Other people experience moments. I notice their expiration dates while they’re still happening.

That’s where the disconnect begins. I notice myself responding differently to things that seem to energize other people. The pull just doesn’t land the same way for me. It’s not easy to explain without turning it into something it isn’t.

I struggle to explain the way I see things without sounding distant or detached. It doesn’t always make sense to other people the way it does in my head. Some parts come across clearly, and some don’t.

And that’s fine. It’s not always easy to fit this perspective into spaces built around different kinds of excitement. I’ve tried to be part of those moments. I’ve smiled in crowded rooms, said the right things, played the role. But while everyone else was celebrating, part of me had already stepped back to watch how automatic it all felt.

People say I overthink. That I should just let go. Maybe they’re right sometimes. But while other people seem able to settle fully into moments, my attention keeps drifting toward the structure underneath them. I notice patterns while moments are still unfolding, and once I see the structure underneath something, it becomes difficult to experience it the same way again.

It’s not that I don’t feel. I just seem to feel things differently. What excites other people doesn’t always affect me the same way. The things that give me peace usually go unnoticed. Maybe that makes me feel invisible in loud rooms. Or maybe I’ve just become more comfortable with quiet.

So I watch. I observe. Not with judgment, just awareness. While people around me move from one moment to the next, I usually find myself standing still a little longer, trying to understand what exists underneath it all.

I’m detached because every moment becomes analysis before it has the chance to become experience.

Before I can fully live something, part of me has already stepped outside of it to understand it instead.

And after a while, that distance changes the experience itself.

I don’t live life. I simulate it.
