Meaning And Attachment

Thoughts from an Agnostic Mind.

Why do humans chase money? Usually, it's to build a better life. But that was never the question that stayed with me. I kept returning to different questions instead. Why do we exist at all? And what happens after death? No one can answer with certainty. I once asked my sister, and she said, "You're either burned to dust, vaporized, or become worm food." She was talking about the body. I was asking about everything that might come after it.

We know death is unavoidable. You take nothing with you, and all you leave behind are the memories you created and the things you once believed mattered. That left me with two ways of looking at death. One is the view my sister holds: that after death, there’s nothing. The other is the perspective Krishna presents: that life is a cycle, and death isn’t the end, but a transition to something beyond.

I'm agnostic. Not because I stopped believing, but because I never found what people seem to find in temples and prayers. Most people arrive there carrying problems. They leave requests. It always looked transactional from the outside, one side asking, the other side silent. I never felt the need to go that way. I never asked them for anything because that kind of relationship never made sense to me.

I don't even pray to them. What I have to say, I say inward. I don't need to fold my hands or chant words. No ritual. No words borrowed from someone else's script. Just whatever's honest when I go quiet enough to hear it. That's my way.

I don't know if I believe any of this. Belief isn't really what keeps pulling me back. I'm more interested in what these ideas reveal about people.

I wasn't trying to become religious. I wanted to understand why people have held on to these beliefs for thousands of years, and why they still do. That curiosity kept leading me back to Krishna's perspective.

Not because I believed in God, but because I had already been asking many of the same questions. I was surprised by how often I arrived at similar ideas on my own.

Krishna describes this human experience as temporary. He describes the body as something we inhabit rather than who we are. If that's true, then what matters isn't what we accumulate, but what remains after everything else falls away. That's why Krishna keeps returning to attachment.

Not because wanting things is wrong, but because clinging to them makes us forget what we are beyond them.

Maybe that's why I keep returning to attachment. The more I watch people, the more it seems like everything eventually becomes something we cling to. Money. Love. Family. Identity. Success. Even suffering.

Maybe what we call meaning is simply the attachment we've decided is worth carrying.

When that attachment disappears, people often say they've "lost themselves." It makes me wonder whether we spend our lives discovering who we are, or slowly becoming everything we're afraid to lose.

If attachment really is what keeps us bound to this life, then I understand why so many traditions speak about letting go before they speak about peace. That keeps bringing me back to one question: if the soul continues beyond this life, why would it return at all?

I don't know. One possibility I keep coming back to is that consciousness carries something unfinished. Not because it is cursed or punished, but because it has not yet learned how to let go. Rebirth, if it exists, might not be a punishment. It could simply be repetition, returning to the same lesson in different forms until something finally understands.

Maybe it’s not the answers we find, but the questions we learn to live with.
