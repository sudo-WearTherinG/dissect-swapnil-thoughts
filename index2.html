Paid In Promises, Living on Hope

Just a Father and His Child Bonding

Most people don’t think of their parents as having been children once. He was one too. That part usually gets ignored.

Ever wondered why he goes through so much to provide for your demands? He fulfills them not as a responsibility, but out of love.

You give that big wide smile when you get what you asked for, that sparkle in the eyes. Maybe fathers are simple that way. That smile still works every time. Maybe he remembers being a kid too, asking his own father for small things, and now he smiles to himself when he sees you do the same.

When he gets his anger out on you, remember it's not you but something happened at work, maybe on the train, or maybe it was just a small buildup of stress somewhere. Just cut him some slack and don't talk back, he'll realize it when he cools down.

He might not say “I love you” every day, but he shows it every time he quietly fixes a problem before you even know it existed. And all he ever asks for in return is that smile. That one smile that makes it all worth it.


You Were Never Meant to Be Rich

You spend 30, maybe 40 years in the same company, day in and day out, giving your time, energy, and health. By the end of it, the expectation often forms that something should have accumulated from it: the nicer car, the bigger house, the freedom? the vacation home, the designer clothes? Shouldn’t loyalty mean something in the end? Shouldn’t sacrifice come back around?

But for many, that doesn’t really happen in a clean, proportional way. The result is usually stability, not transformation.

Over time, the early idea that consistent effort naturally turns into large rewards starts to feel less certain. Outcomes depend on wages, cost of living, timing, and how limited salary-based growth actually is over long stretches of time.

But no, the system was never designed to make most people rich. It runs on labor: steady, reliable, replaceable labor. People put in their time, energy, and skill, and a portion of the value created is captured as profit elsewhere. In return, they get stability and wages that keep things moving. It’s designed more to keep things functioning day to day than to completely change where most people end up.

The system does not require you to live well, only to remain functional within it. Promotions begin feeling less like rewards and more like reasons to keep moving. Benefits? Just enough to stop you from getting tired or quitting. And when you can’t work anymore, there is no real closure. They give you a small pension, maybe a handshake, and then move on the next day.

You spend years believing hard work automatically becomes freedom. But time has limits. You can only work so many hours, sacrifice so much energy, trade so much of yourself before you reach a ceiling. And many people spend decades climbing only to discover the ladder was leaning against survival and keeping life running, not freedom.

Maybe that's because people are playing different games. Some sell time for stability. Others risk stability trying to build ownership. One offers predictability. The other offers uncertainty. One can protect you from falling. The other can let you climb higher, or lose everything.

The strange part is that many people spend their whole lives believing both paths eventually lead to the same destination.


Paying Taxes, Getting Nothing in Return

The idea is simple. You pay into the system, and the system is supposed to work for you. Healthcare. Education. Roads. Clean water. Basic things that shouldn't require negotiation.

But the return rarely matches what was promised.

You walk into a government hospital with someone you love and discover there are no beds. Medicines are out of stock. You wait anyway, because there is nowhere else to go that won't cost more than you have. In schools, children are told to dream big while sitting on broken benches with outdated books, taught by teachers who are themselves underpaid and stretched thin.

Outside, the roads tell the same story. The infrastructure holds together just enough to keep things moving, rarely enough to make things better.

And so people pay twice. Once to the government through taxes, income, GST, tolls, fuel. And again to private hospitals, private schools, private water suppliers because the public version isn't holding.

The money leaves. The services don't arrive.

Your taxes don't build your future. They maintain their empire.

You're Like A Fucking Rabbit Who Breeds For Them

You spend years working, paying taxes, doing your part. Study. Work. Earn. Repeat. Life keeps moving, and eventually the routine starts feeling normal. Then one day you get married, have children, and slowly begin teaching them the same things you were taught. Work hard. Stay safe. Follow the path.

Not because you want to trap them. Usually because you love them. Parents hand down what they believe will protect their children. But people don’t only pass down love. They also pass down fears, routines, expectations, and ways of living they never had time to question themselves.

And maybe that’s how the cycle survives. Not through force. Not because someone controls every step. But because each generation quietly teaches the next how to survive inside the world they inherited. Then those children grow up and do the same.

Maybe exhaustion does something strange to people. When you're constantly busy, tired, and trying to survive, you stop having energy left for bigger questions.

And somewhere in all of this, children arrive. The system doesn't announce itself. It doesn't need to. It simply waits for the next generation to be born into the world the previous one normalized. Love handles the transmission. Exhaustion handles the rest.

You know what the real problem is? You still don’t get it. You’re just reading this, not understanding. You think you're in control, but you keep following routines you never consciously chose. Wake up. Work. Pay. Repeat. Like cargo moving from one destination to another, convinced it picked the route itself. And maybe that's the trick. The cage works best when people stop seeing the bars.

It convinces you that survival is living, and that living is rebellion.

The World Doesn't Feel The Same

Sometimes it feels like everyone learned a rhythm I somehow missed. People move through life naturally while I keep stopping to stare at the machinery underneath it. Life starts to look repetitive and mechanical, and I can’t tell if I’m seeing something others aren’t or just stuck in my own head. It gets isolating, like I’m separated from everyone else even when I’m surrounded by them.
