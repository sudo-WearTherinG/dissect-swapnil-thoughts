The Lie We Call Life

Trapped in a System Built by Them, For Them!

These people keep asking me, “Why don't you have a job?” And I just smile, because if I speak, they won't get it. Still, let me say it for once.

Why don't you ask yourself, “Why am I living like this?”

Let me break it down.

You wake up at 6 or 7 a.m. Take a bath in a hurry, eat something, and then it begins.

Run to catch a rickshaw. Fight to get in the train. Stand like cattle. Reach the office.

Work like a machine from dawn to dusk. The same typing, the same files, the same endless routine.

Your time, your skills, your mental peace, all directed at the company, which barely notices your presence.

After work, again the same routine. Train, rickshaw, traffic, noise. Reach home at 8 or 9 p.m. Eat. Watch TV for 30 minutes. Sleep.

And then repeat.

Somewhere along the way, you began believing that busyness meant purpose.

You started treating exhaustion like proof that you were doing life correctly.

It slowly stops feeling like life and becomes repetition.

And what do you have to show after 20 or 30 years of this?

A second-hand car? A 1BHK flat with EMI running till you're 60? Maybe a fridge, a cooler, a TV bought on discount.

You don't own your time. Your body is tired. Your dreams? Forgotten. You just become someone who's “doing his duty.”

That's the reward? For decades of sacrifice?

Mother's script

And this loop isn't just for men. Women have a script too.

Wake up before everyone, cook for husband and kids, pack tiffin. Clean. Drop kids. Do housework. Serve lunch. Maybe rest for an hour. Then tea time, evening snacks, kids' homework, groceries. Make dinner. Serve husband. Clean again. Sleep.

No weekends. No salary. No “thank you.” Just expectations.

She does it all, for her husband, her kids, for everyone she gives her life to.

Over time, expectations become so normal that her effort disappears into routine. Meals appear. Clothes get folded. The house keeps moving. Because everything continues working, people slowly stop noticing the work itself.

Be a “good wife,” a “good mother.” Always smiling, never tired. And repeat.

They say “family is everything,” but over time her life quietly becomes organized around everyone else's needs.

Somewhere in all of this, a simple question rarely gets asked:

“Is she even living?”

And maybe that's the quieter truth. To spend years giving your life to others, while slowly growing tired down to her bones.

From Birth - Trained to Work, Not to Think!

You're taught to survive, not always to question.

And maybe that's the strange part.

Some questions never make it into classrooms.

Our schools don't teach how to think. They teach how to obey.

Sit straight. Don't talk. Memorize. Pass the exam. Become a good boy, good girl, and later, a good worker.

You weren’t taught to sell your time. You were taught to get a job.

Like that was noble. Like that was freedom.

You spend years helping larger systems grow, while sometimes struggling to build stability in your own life.

You keep showing up, hoping things eventually begin making sense. Sometimes they do. Sometimes the routine simply continues.

You’re part of systems that could continue moving without you tomorrow.

No one teaches you about life. About mental health. About money. About freedom.

Just: “Do your job, settle down, have kids, be thankful, and reproduce more workers, just like you.”

You think getting 30k or 50k per month is success? For many people, it barely feels like breathing room.

The moment income stops, life can begin shaking faster than people expect. For many people, stability depends on continuing the cycle.

For many people, that's what a job slowly becomes. Not a career. A drug.

You can see it in the evenings sometimes. The tired eyes. The silence on the ride home. People returning with just enough energy left to repeat tomorrow.

You say, “But what else can we do?”

Some beliefs become so normal they stop feeling like beliefs at all.

Ideas like: you're easily replaced, powerless, or that stepping outside the script automatically means failure.

But following the script doesn't always create the freedom people expect. Large systems don't always reward thinkers. They often reward predictability.

People realize, sooner or later, how replaceable they can feel inside large systems.

One leave application and they're already training someone else. Work moves on surprisingly fast. Companies move. Systems move. Individuals often realize they mattered less to the structure than they imagined.

That desk, that system, that ID card. It's not yours. It never was.

Over time, many people begin organizing their lives around expectations that aren't entirely their own.

If you work too much, they call you responsible. If you rest or think different, they call you lazy.

Then there is language.

Many Indian youth learn just enough English to enter workplaces. Enough for meetings, instructions, and routine. The language of survival often arrives before the language of self-expression.

They don't learn communication. They learn survival phrases.

Slowly, fluency began looking a lot like intelligence. The two aren't always the same thing.

So when people ask, “Why don't you have a job?” I sometimes wonder if they're asking something else.

Maybe: Why aren't you following the routine everyone else follows?

I don't think the question comes from cruelty.

Little by little, busyness began looking like purpose, and repetition began looking like life.

Maybe I'm wrong. But I keep feeling like life was meant for more than waking up tired just to earn the right to repeat tomorrow.

I've spent too much time standing at the edges, watching the routine repeat itself.

We Kill Child Innocence To Raise The Adult!

This is where I introduce you to your children's cycle.

When they're small, you let them be. Running around barefoot in dusty lanes, chasing pigeons, laughing over nothing. They draw on walls, break things, ask endless questions. You call it “masti,” and you smile.

But the moment their fingers can hold a pencil firmly, everything changes.

You stop calling it innocence. Now it's “focus.” You stop listening to their silly stories. Now it's “study time.” You stop celebrating their curiosity. Now it's “learn what's in the syllabus.”

You sit them down and begin selling them dreams. Not their dreams, but yours. Engineer. Doctor. IAS. Something “respectable.”

You think you're doing it out of love. But what you really did was kill the most human part of them. Their innocence.

Now, the house transforms into an extension of the school. Every table turns into a study desk, and every conversation becomes a lecture.

And when your child comes close for a hug, it comes with fear. Because the hug might turn into another lecture on “How life works” or “How hard success is.”

This is where your child learns:

Love has conditions. Approval must be earned. Rest is laziness. And life? It's not to be lived. It's to be passed like an exam.

This is how you break a free soul, and train them to become exactly what you became.

You did everything they told you. Listened to your father. Followed the script.

Study hard. Get a job. Get married. Have kids.

Be a responsible man. Be a good husband. Don't question, just provide.

All the Time, But No Strength to Use It at 60-65.

Now you're retired. The world says you've earned your rest.

But today, as you sit at the dining table, there's a noticeable stillness in the air. No rush. No meetings. No deadlines. Just a peaceful quiet.

You look around.

Your son is getting ready for office. Same tired eyes, same fast breakfast, same silence.

Your daughter is juggling a laptop, her tea, and time. She's already late.

And then you glance at your wife. She's aged. The lines on her face weren't there before.

The same hands that once held yours now move through the morning chores. Like she's still on the clock.

And suddenly, it hits you.

You didn't escape anything. You just passed it down.

You spent your life doing what your father said. And now your children are doing what you did.

Same loop. Same trap. Just with faster phones and newer EMIs.

You always thought one day you'd break free. Or maybe they would.

But here you are. Watching your own children walk into the same machine.

And for the first time in years, your heart feels heavy. Not for yourself, but for them.

You want to stop them. You want to say, “This isn't living.”

But you don't.

Because eventually, even your voice got tired.

So you just sit there… and sip your tea.

And later that evening, you glance into the mirror.

For a second, you don't recognize yourself.

The black hair is mostly gone. The eyes are dull. The skin feels unfamiliar.

You whisper to your reflection: After all that... what exactly was exchanged for all that time? What do I have? Retirement savings? Where did my youth go?
