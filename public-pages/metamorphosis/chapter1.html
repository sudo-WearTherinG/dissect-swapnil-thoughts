Chapter I

The Wrong Shape

This is it, then. This is what he's supposed to call his life.

He doesn’t need permission. He doesn’t need anyone’s approval. There’s structure, apparently. He just can’t feel where it starts or why it matters. He watches it quietly, a pattern no one else seems to notice.

Something shifts inside him. Questions rise and disappear faster than answers. The future is there, but it doesn’t look like what people say it should be. Work. Success. Proving himself. All of it feels empty.

Each thought pulls him further from the ordinary. From the rules that say living means following them.

He notices something strange. Not in the world, but in what he wants from it.

Things that once felt normal don’t pull at him anymore. Not because he hates them. Just because they don’t reach him. Like someone turned down the volume and forgot to turn it back.

Around him, people move as if something invisible is pulling them forward. They seem to know what they’re chasing. Or they act like they do. He tries to feel the same urgency, but it doesn’t come. It feels learned. Repeated. Not real.

He saw it everywhere once he noticed it.

On the train, people stood before the station even arrived. Bags already lifted. Eyes already waiting near the doors. Their bodies moved a second ahead of thought, as if tomorrow had rehearsed them in advance.

He wondered when everyone else had learned the timing.

And why does he feel like one day he'll wake up inside it and realize he never noticed himself becoming part of it?

What stays instead is curiosity. Quiet, constant. Not about success. Not about winning. Just… questions. Why this life? Why this mind? Why now, and not some other time, some other form?

He doesn't feel above it. He doesn't feel outside it. Just slightly misaligned.

Like he was placed into something already moving, and everyone else learned the rhythm faster than him.

It shows when people ask what he does.

Family gatherings were the worst for it.

Someone always asked eventually.

Between bites of food. Between harmless questions.

Casual enough that nobody noticed the weight of it.

He understands the question before it finishes.

Not just what it means, but where it leads.

The kind of answer it wants. The kind that fits cleanly into their world.

He knows he could give them a label.

He also knows it wouldn't mean anything to him.

And anything that would mean something to him wouldn't make sense to them.

So he pauses, not because he's thinking about it. He already knows what he'll say.

The pause lasts only a second. Maybe less. Long enough for someone to glance at him, then away.

The question isn't really new. It never is. It always comes the same way, and it always expects something simple back.

He gives them that. Something that works. Something that doesn't need explaining.

Heads nod. Someone reaches for another dish. The conversation keeps moving.

They take it and move on. That's enough for them. It always is.

The answer stays in his mind for a moment. It sounds normal. It makes sense.

By tomorrow he probably wouldn't remember the answer he gave. Only the feeling of relief after it ended.

The answer had done its job. That was all it had needed to do.

It doesn't feel like his anymore, like it belongs to a version of him that only exists for other people.
