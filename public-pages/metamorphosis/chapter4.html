Chapter IV

He Was A Human

This room was never built for questions like his.

He has never said any of it out loud.

Not because the words don't exist. He has more words than he knows what to do with. Pages of them. But words need a room they can survive in. And this room was built for different conversations.

He looks at his father and tries to locate the opening. The moment where he could say, not the argument, not the philosophy, just the plain thing underneath all of it. That existing inside this skin feels like wearing something cut for someone else. That the world everyone moves through with such certainty feels like a stage he wandered onto without being given the script. That some mornings the weight of being conscious is more than he knows what to do with.

He tries to find the sentence that would make this land.

There isn't one.

Not because his father is cruel. But because his father lives inside the script completely. Not performing it. Actually inside it. The job, the duty, the forward motion, these are not questions for him. They are the answer. They have always been the answer.

And you cannot explain the question to someone who never felt it.

He wants to ask just once.

The life you are telling me to follow.

I have seen the people already inside it.

I watched them on the train. In the office. At the dinner table.

I watched them carry things back to a place that never changes.

They don't look free. They don't look arrived.

They look like they are still waiting for the part where it makes sense.

So what exactly am I supposed to be moving toward?

Because every direction felt like being told to keep moving by people who still looked lost themselves.

He doesn't ask.

Because he already knows what would happen. The script has an answer for this too. Something about patience. About dues being paid. About how meaning comes later, after the work, after the sacrifice, after you have proven yourself to a system that will replace you quietly and without ceremony.

He has read that answer before. In the eyes of every tired person he has ever watched from the bench.

So he sits across from his father in the particular silence of someone who cannot be understood and has stopped expecting otherwise.

Not with anger. Just with the quiet recognition that some distances cannot be crossed. That some gaps exist not because people don't love each other. But because they cannot reach each other across what they each believe life is for.

And that is its own kind of loneliness.

The kind with no villain.

The kind that just sits there quietly.

The kind that looks exactly like an ordinary evening.
