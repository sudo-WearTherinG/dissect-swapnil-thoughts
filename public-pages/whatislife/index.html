I Was Never Here

Looking Down, Seeing Through.
Sometimes, all you need to do is sit still and watch.


I decided to sit on a bench. That’s all. Just sit and watch. What I saw wasn’t anything new. Just people living. Running around, talking, carrying bags, feeding kids, scrolling on phones, laughing, shouting, surviving. Everyone chasing something, even if they don’t know what.

I saw how a baby slowly becomes a boy. Then one day, the boy becomes a man. Then that man starts carrying more than just his own weight. Bills. Guilt. Expectations. He becomes responsible for someone else’s dreams. Then comes marriage, not always because he wanted to, but because it was time. That’s how it goes. One role after another, until you forget who you were before all the roles began.

Even in all that mess, they still find joy. A man smiles at a message on his phone. A kid jumps in muddy water like it is the best game in the world. A mother steals one quiet sip of tea before the next chore calls. Small things, a joke, a song on the radio, a cool breeze after a hot day. They do not fix anything, but they keep you breathing. Little pockets of air in a life that tries to drown you. Maybe that is why people keep going, not because life gets easier, but because these moments remind them they are alive.

But that is the saddest part. These small joys are all we get. A laugh here, a meal there, one quiet evening that does not feel like a burden. We hold on to them like scraps, pretending they make up for everything else. The stress, the noise, the pressure, they never disappear. It just hides behind those short moments of peace. We move forward, telling ourselves, “At least I smiled today.” But deep down, we know. This is not how it was meant to be. We were supposed to live, not just function. Be free, not just busy. Somewhere along the way, survival became the goal, and no one remembers what freedom looked like.

Somewhere between all the noise, and the noise we’ve learned to call life, we forgot to ask the real questions. Not that anyone’s asking now.

It’s not just what they do. It’s what they tell themselves while doing it. The quiet negotiations no one hears: “Just a few more years.” “This is worth it.” “Everyone lives like this.” Not lies exactly. Just softened versions of the truth, enough to keep moving, not enough to make them stop and look too closely. They feel the weight. They notice the repetition. But they smooth it over before it becomes something real, something that won’t let them return to living the same way. Because once it becomes real, it changes everything.

I wondered what would happen if someone did ask. Not the easy questions people expect, but the kind that makes you hesitate before answering. Where is your species going, and where is all this effort taking you? I imagined the pause, the brief discomfort, the way people might reach for answers that sound like direction instead of truth. Work. Family. Plans. Later. Words that keep things moving. I didn’t ask. Some questions don’t want answers; they want space. And no one ever seems to have enough of that.

It makes sense. That’s what makes it worse. The world is built for this kind of survival, and you learned how to live inside it instead of questioning it. And that’s all it becomes.

I know one thing. I don’t want this life. I want to understand what all of this is for.

And I kept sitting. Watching. Now not just them, but myself. Wondering how we got so good at surviving, but so bad at living.

I sit here like I’m above it all, like I’m just watching. But I’m part of this too. I breathe the same air, carry the same weight. I laugh at the same dumb jokes just to feel something. Maybe I just learned how to fake it better. I check my phone the same way, laugh the same way, waste my time the same way. I just gave my emptiness better words.

I don’t know.

I just know I haven’t found a reason yet, and I’m starting to wonder if one was ever there. Until then, I’ll keep sitting. Watching. Trying to see through the noise.

Just a bit longer.

— Observing life, questioning mine —
